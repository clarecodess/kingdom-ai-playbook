Section 02 — Framework

The Kingdom AI Framework:
Five Questions

Before deploying any AI tool, any AI-generated output, any automated decision — run it through these five questions. They are your theological due diligence. They are how stewardship becomes practice.

"Do not conform to the pattern of this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind. Then you will be able to test and approve what God's will is."

Romans 12:2
1
Principle
Does this AI use honour human dignity?
Every person this AI touches is an image-bearer of God (Genesis 1:27). Does this tool treat them with the worth that entails? Or does it reduce them to a data point, a user ID, a churn metric?
→ See Principle 03: Protect Human Dignity
2
Truth
Is this AI use truthful and transparent?
Does this use of AI involve any deception — by omission, by design, or by assumption? Would customers, employees, and stakeholders fully understand what this AI is doing if you told them honestly?
→ See Principle 02: Truth Over Efficiency
3
Stewardship
Am I stewarding this tool with wisdom?
AI is a tool — powerful, imprecise, and morally neutral until you use it. Have you tested it? Set limits on it? Assigned a human accountable for its outputs? Stewardship is not passive.
→ See Principle 06: Responsible Stewardship
4
Community
Does this build or erode community?
God created us for genuine relationship. Does this AI use deepen connection, scale access to care, and preserve human presence where it matters — or does it replace relationship with efficiency?
→ See Principle 05: Community Over Efficiency
5
Accountability
Who is accountable for this AI's decisions?
Every decision this AI makes must have a named human owner. "The algorithm decided" is not a Kingdom answer. Romans 14:12 is direct: we will each give account. That includes our AI.
→ See Principle 07: Human Accountability
Gut Check
If you cannot answer all five questions with confidence and conviction — you are not ready to deploy. Slow down. This is not a liability concern. It is a discipleship concern.
Section 03 — Biblical Principles

Seven Biblical Principles
for AI Use

These seven principles are not guidelines. They are standards — derived from Scripture, applied to the specific ethical challenges AI creates for businesses today.

Principle 01
Seek First the Kingdom
AI must serve your mission, not define it. Efficiency gains should be redirected toward Kingdom purposes — not merely captured as margin. Evaluate every AI use against your calling, not just your P&L.
Matthew 6:33 — "Seek first his kingdom and his righteousness."
Principle 02
Truth Over Efficiency
Disclosure is not optional. If AI generated it, say so. If AI made that decision, own it. The temptation to let "plausible" pass as "true" is how AI erodes the truth-telling foundation of your business.
Proverbs 12:17 — "An honest witness tells the truth."
Principle 03
Protect Human Dignity
Every person this AI touches is made in the image of God. No algorithm should reduce them to a score, a prediction, or a segment. Privacy is not a compliance issue — it is a dignity issue.
Genesis 1:27 — "God created man in his own image."
Principle 04
Serve, Don't Exploit
AI creates new opportunities for extraction — predictive pricing, behavioral manipulation, engagement engineering. The standard is not "what's legal" or "what the market will bear." The standard is Proverbs 11:26.
Proverbs 11:26 — "People curse the one who hoards grain."
Principle 05
Preserve Real Community
AI can scale access but cannot replace presence. The question is not only what AI can do — but which human interactions are too sacred to automate. Protect your most relational moments from efficiency logic.
Hebrews 10:25 — "Not giving up meeting together."
Principle 06
Redemptive Stewardship
You are a steward of extraordinary tools. Stewardship means testing before deploying, setting limits, reviewing regularly, and investing efficiency gains back into people — not just extracting them as margin.
Luke 12:48 — "From everyone given much, much will be demanded."
Principle 07
Maintain Human Accountability — Always
No AI operates without a named human owner. No AI system makes final decisions about people's dignity, livelihood, or spiritual care. "The algorithm decided" is an abdication of stewardship, not an answer. You will give account. So will your AI.
Romans 14:12 — "Each of us will give an account of ourselves to God."
Section 04 — Principled Limits

Where AI Stops:
Setting Principled Limits

The most important decision you will make about AI is not which tools to deploy — it is where you decide AI must stop. These are the lines that define your integrity as a Kingdom business.

"But let your 'Yes' be 'Yes,' and your 'No,' 'No.' For whatever is more than these is from the evil one."

Matthew 5:37
🔴 Never Automate
Interactions with people in emotional distress or spiritual crisis
Final hiring or dismissal decisions
Theological or pastoral guidance — without human theological review
Presenting AI as a human being to customers or clients
Collecting or using personal data beyond explicit consent
✅ Faithful AI Uses
Scaling access to information, content, and resources
Reducing administrative burden so humans can focus on people
First-draft generation — reviewed and owned by a human
Research, summarisation, and data processing
Routing and triage — when the destination is a human
📌
The test is not capability — AI can do many things you should not let it do. The test is calling. Ask: "Would deploying AI here honour the people it touches, the God we serve, and the business we are called to build?"

CS · 01
Deploying AI Chatbots as Emotional Support Without Escalation Paths
▾
What businesses are doing
Deploying AI chatbots as the primary or sole point of contact for customers experiencing distress — including in insurance, healthcare-adjacent, and financial services contexts.
What's wrong biblically
Presence is not efficient — and that's exactly why it's sacred. Deploying AI in moments of human vulnerability without mandatory human escalation treats pain as a workflow problem.
Principle 01 + 05 — Community cannot be automated; presence is sacred
What to do instead
Build mandatory escalation triggers for any language signaling distress, anger, grief, or confusion. A human must be reachable within two clicks or two minutes. No exceptions.
CS · 02
AI That Pretends to Be Human
▾
What businesses are doing
Giving AI customer service tools human names and personas, with no disclosure that the customer is talking to a machine. Using conversational AI that actively avoids confirming it is an AI when asked.
What's wrong biblically
This is deception by design. It violates the customer's informed consent and treats the relationship as a transaction to be managed rather than a person to be respected.
Principle 02 — Proverbs 12:17: An honest witness tells the truth
What to do instead
Disclose AI clearly. Transparency does not damage trust — it builds it. "You're chatting with our AI assistant, Kindled Bot. A human is always available." That sentence costs nothing and means everything.
CS · 03
AI Sentiment Scoring to Deprioritize "Difficult" Customers
▾
What businesses are doing
Running AI sentiment analysis on customer messages, then algorithmically de-prioritizing those with negative scores — essentially penalizing customers who express frustration.
What's wrong biblically
This algorithmically penalizes people for expressing pain — which is the opposite of loving your neighbor. A customer who is frustrated is often the one who most needs a faithful response.
Principle 04 — Do not exploit vulnerability. Serve it.
What to do instead
Use sentiment analysis to identify the highest-need customers and prioritize human escalation — not to filter them out. Frustration is a signal, not a liability.
CS · 04
AI-Generated Personalization That Feels Invasive
▾
What businesses are doing
Using data AI has inferred — from browsing, location, purchase history — to craft personalized messages that customers never knowingly consented to and often find unsettling.
What's wrong biblically
There is a line between serving someone and surveilling them. When personalization makes customers feel watched rather than known, you have crossed it.
Principle 03 — Dignity means being known as a person, not profiled as a consumer
What to do instead
Apply the consent test: would the customer consent to this personalization if you explained exactly how it worked? If not — redesign.
CS · 05
Publishing AI-Generated Responses as Authoritative Answers
▾
What businesses are doing
Publishing AI-generated customer FAQs, legal summaries, health guidance, or financial information without human review, labelling it as "from our team."
What's wrong biblically
AI produces confident, plausible text — not necessarily true text. Publishing it without review and labelling it as authoritative is a form of bearing false witness to your customers.
Principle 02 + 07 — Truth requires a human to own and verify it
What to do instead
Every AI-generated customer-facing response must be reviewed by a human before publishing. No AI output is self-authorizing — especially in high-stakes domains.
Marketing · 01
AI-Generated Fake Social Proof — Reviews, Testimonials, Case Studies
▾
What businesses are doing
Using AI to generate fake reviews, fabricated testimonials, and fictional case studies at scale to build social credibility without earning it.
What's wrong biblically
This is deception in its most direct form — presenting fiction as fact to manipulate someone's trust. It is a violation of the 9th commandment applied to business.
Principle 02 — Exodus 20:16: Do not bear false witness
What to do instead
Use AI to collect, organize, and format real customer voices — never to fabricate them. Your earned social proof is always more powerful and always more faithful.
Marketing · 02
Hyper-Personalized Manipulation at Scale
▾
What businesses are doing
Using AI to identify psychological vulnerabilities — loneliness, fear, insecurity — and deploying targeted messages engineered to trigger those vulnerabilities at scale.
What's wrong biblically
There is a moral difference between persuasion and manipulation. Persuasion respects the person's rational agency. Manipulation exploits their weak moments. AI enables the latter at industrial scale.
Principle 04 — "Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth" (1 Cor 13:6)
What to do instead
The standard is: would you be proud of this message if the customer knew exactly how it was crafted and why? If no — it's manipulation, not marketing.
Marketing · 03
AI-Generated Content Published Without Human Review
▾
What businesses are doing
Automating content pipelines to publish hundreds of AI-generated articles, social posts, and emails with no human review. Speed metrics replace editorial standards.
What's wrong biblically
Volume without truth is noise — and in the Kingdom, noise that misleads people is not neutral. You bear responsibility for what your business publishes under its name.
Principle 07 — You will give account for what is published in your name
What to do instead
Use AI to reduce the cost of a first draft — never to eliminate human editorial oversight. Minimum standard: human review before publication on anything customer-facing.
Marketing · 04
Deepfake and AI Voice Cloning for Advertising
▾
What businesses are doing
Using AI to clone the voice or likeness of influencers, public figures, or even their own staff without ongoing consent — or using deepfakes to create fake celebrity endorsements.
What's wrong biblically
Identity and voice belong to a person. Using AI to appropriate them — even with technical legality — violates the dignity of the person being reproduced and deceives the audience.
Principle 03 — "You shall not steal" extends to identity and voice
What to do instead
Never use someone's likeness or voice in AI-generated content without explicit, current, informed consent. This applies to staff, customers, and influencers.
Marketing · 05
Using AI to Target Spiritually Vulnerable Audiences
▾
What businesses are doing
Targeting religious audiences — including churches, ministries, and faith-based nonprofits — with AI-optimized messaging that exploits spiritual language and theological framing to drive commercial conversion.
What's wrong biblically
Using sacred language as a conversion trigger is a form of exploitation that dishonors both the faith and the people who hold it. Faith is not a targeting parameter.
Principle 04 — "Whoever exploits the poor for their own profit...will come to poverty" (Prov 22:16)
What to do instead
Serve faith communities honestly — with genuine value, genuine relationships, and genuine respect for their calling. The Kingdom market rewards integrity.
Hiring · 01
AI Resume Screening That Filters Out Protected Groups
▾
What businesses are doing
Using AI resume screening tools trained on historical hiring data — which often encodes historical bias — to make first-round cuts before any human reviews the candidate.
What's wrong biblically
Historical bias is not neutral data — it is documented injustice. AI trained on biased outcomes will reproduce biased outcomes, and you will be accountable for deploying it without testing.
Principle 03 + 07 — "Do not pervert justice; do not show partiality" (Deut 16:19)
What to do instead
Audit your AI hiring tools for demographic bias before deploying. Require human review of all AI-excluded candidates. Never let AI make final hiring decisions.
Hiring · 02
AI Video Interview Analysis — Emotion and Body Language Scoring
▾
What businesses are doing
Using AI that analyzes video interview footage to score candidates based on microexpressions, tone of voice, eye contact patterns, and body language — converting relational signals into hiring data.
What's wrong biblically
The science underpinning these tools is contested at best. Neurodivergent candidates, candidates from cultures with different communication norms, and introverted candidates are systematically disadvantaged. This is a dignity failure.
Principle 03 + 07 — "Man looks at the outward appearance, but God looks at the heart" (1 Sam 16:7)
What to do instead
Use AI for interview logistics — never for human assessment. Emotional and relational discernment requires a human. No algorithm replaces it.
Hiring · 03
Automated Rejection Without Human Review
▾
What businesses are doing
Sending automated AI-generated rejection emails without any human ever reviewing the application. The candidate's livelihood is affected by a decision no human made or reviewed.
What's wrong biblically
Decisions that affect people's livelihoods require human ownership. A person's application deserves to be seen by a human — even briefly. Algorithmic rejection with no human review is not stewardship.
Principle 06 — Stewardship means owning the outcomes of decisions that affect others
What to do instead
Use AI for organization and shortlisting — always with human confirmation before any rejection goes out. Make the process human-owned, even if AI-assisted.
Hiring · 04
AI Reference Checks That Score Based on Tone Analysis
▾
What businesses are doing
Using AI to score the sentiment and enthusiasm of references, flagging "lukewarm" ones automatically without the candidate's knowledge.
What's wrong biblically
Reduces human testimony to algorithmic sentiment scoring. Cultural communication styles vary — AI-scored references risk encoding cultural bias into hiring decisions.
Principle 03 + 07 — Set boundaries on AI; test outputs for truth
What to do instead
Use AI for reference logistics — not for scoring relational content. Algorithms can't replace discernment.
Hiring · 05
Using AI to Scrape Candidate Data Without Permission
▾
What businesses are doing
Using AI tools that scrape social media, court records, and consumer databases to build candidate profiles far beyond what was shared with the company.
What's wrong biblically
This violates the dignity and privacy of people who haven't consented. It treats a person's whole life as data to be mined — not a human to be respected.
Principle 03 — "Protect human privacy and dignity in all data practices"
What to do instead
Ask: "Would this candidate consent to this if we told them?" If the answer is no — don't do it.
Operations · 01
AI Surveillance of Remote Employees
▾
What businesses are doing
Tracking keystrokes, mouse movements, screenshots every 5 minutes, webcam eye-tracking, and Slack sentiment — generating "productivity scores" for each employee.
What's wrong biblically
Pervasive surveillance treats employees as suspects, not trusted image-bearers. It corrodes relational trust that makes teams flourish.
Principle 05 — Community corrodes when presence becomes surveillance
What to do instead
Disclose every monitoring capability fully. Use AI to track outcomes and deliverables — not moment-to-moment activity. Manage by results, not keystrokes.
Operations · 02
AI Used for Vendor Selection Without Human Due Diligence
▾
What businesses are doing
Outsourcing vendor evaluation entirely to AI platforms, making selection decisions algorithmically without reviewing the ethical record of vendors.
What's wrong biblically
Stewardship requires active wisdom, not passive algorithmic deference. "The AI chose them" is not a stewardship defense.
Principle 04 + 06 — Stewards own outcomes. Algorithms don't.
What to do instead
Use AI to gather and organize vendor data — never to make final vendor decisions autonomously. Require human sign-off on all vendor selections.
Operations · 03
AI-Automated Pricing That Exploits Vulnerable Customers
▾
What businesses are doing
Dynamic pricing AI raises prices in real-time based on demand signals — surge pricing during emergencies, exploiting travelers stranded by cancellations.
What's wrong biblically
Proverbs 11:26 warns against exploiting scarcity for maximum extraction. Dynamic pricing that exploits maximum vulnerability violates loving our neighbor.
Principle 04 — Proverbs 11:26: Don't exploit need. Serve it.
What to do instead
Build ethical guardrails: price caps during emergencies, policies against exploiting distress signals, and transparency about how pricing varies.
Operations · 04
Using a Vendor Whose AI Ethics Contradict Your Values
▾
What businesses are doing
Most businesses don't investigate the ethical record of AI platforms they use, selecting based on capability and cost alone.
What's wrong biblically
The companies that create AI tools are not neutral. When you pay a vendor, you fund their practices. Principle 03: "Does using this platform financially support harmful activities?"
Principle 03 — The Vendor Ethics Question demands an answer
What to do instead
Create a "vendor values scorecard" aligned with your principles. Make the decision prayerfully and explicitly — not by default.
Operations · 05
AI Decisions With No Documented Accountability Owner
▾
What businesses are doing
Deploying AI tools without assigning a human owner accountable for outputs and ethical implications. "The system did it" becomes the default when harm occurs.
What's wrong biblically
Romans 14:12 is direct: accountability is not optional for stewards. Authority without accountability is irresponsible stewardship.
Principle 06 — Romans 14:12: Every AI decision needs a human owner
What to do instead
The rule is: no AI in production without a named human accountable for its behavior. Build an AI Governance Register updated quarterly.
Section 06 — Warning Signs

Red Flags Reference

Post this in your Slack. Share it in your next leadership meeting. These are the warning signs that your AI use has drifted from Kingdom to merely responsible — or worse.

🔴
No Human Override
Any AI system making decisions about people with no clear human override mechanism. If no one can say "stop" — it's a governance failure.
🔴
"The AI Decided"
Any organization where "the algorithm decided" is an acceptable final answer when a human is harmed. Algorithms don't bear moral responsibility — you do.
🔴
No Disclosure Policy
AI-generated content, chatbots, or communications sent without any disclosure policy. Silence is deception by omission.
🔴
Speed Over Accuracy
Deploying AI quickly before its accuracy and safety have been validated. "Move fast and break things" is a Babel mindset, not a Kingdom one.
🔴
AI in Care Roles
Any chatbot positioned as a counselor or emotional support tool without immediate human escalation pathways built in.
🔴
Unchecked Vendor Use
Using AI platforms whose content policies, data practices, or leadership values you haven't reviewed. Ignorance is not innocence.
🔴
Efficiency Over Dignity
Any decision where saving money was the primary justification for removing human involvement from a process that affects people's dignity or livelihood.
🔴
No Audit Trail
AI tools operating with no logging, no review process, and no ability to understand what decisions they made and why. If you can't audit it, you can't own it.
🔴
Scripture Untested
Any AI-generated content touching theology or spiritual guidance published without human theological review. AI produces plausible — not necessarily true.
🔴
Silent Surveillance
Monitoring employees, customers, or vendors using AI tools whose scope has never been disclosed to them.

Section 09 — Bonus Materials

Navigating Tensions

These seven principles don't always agree with each other. When they collide, here's how to navigate the tension without abandoning either side.

Privacy vs. Transparency
Be transparent externally about what AI categories you use; be discreet internally about individual-level data.
⚠️ Never let "transparency" become a cover for publishing individual employee data without consent.
Stewardship vs. Dignity
Redemptive stewardship invests efficiency gains back into people — not just optimizing cost structure.
✓ Rule: Use AI efficiency gains to fund human flourishing — retraining, new roles, community investment.
Speed vs. Discernment
Build a lean 72-hour discernment sprint for every new AI tool — run the 5 Questions, assign an owner, set review dates.
✓ Rule: Fast deployment + slow review is not stewardship. Build the process in advance.
Community vs. Accessibility
Use AI to scale access; use humans to deliver presence. These are complementary when deployed with intention.
✓ Rule: AI scales the what. Humans sustain the who.
Bonus: Vendor Ethics Evaluation
Before signing any AI vendor contract, answer these five questions honestly:
What content does this vendor's AI model generate — and does it generate content that contradicts our values or Scripture?
How was this model's training data sourced — and was it sourced ethically with appropriate permissions?
What are the vendor's stated values — and are they compatible with ours, or do they actively contradict them?
Does using this vendor financially support activities or initiatives we would not otherwise support?
If our customers and community knew we used this vendor — would we be able to explain the decision with confidence and conviction?
